How to Play

Overview

The goal

Mahjong is a four-player strategy game built on pattern recognition and timing. Each player draws and discards tiles to build a complete hand before anyone else does.

There are many variants of mahjong, often named after the region that popularized them — for example, Hong Kong Old Style and American Mah Jongg. Our club plays a Japanese variant called riichi, named for a move you can make during the game.

What a winning hand looks like

A standard winning hand has 14 tiles arranged as four sets and one pair. Understanding this shape is the foundation of everything else in the game.

  • A sequence is three consecutive numbered tiles of the same suit — for example, 4–5–6 or 1–2–3.
  • A triplet is three identical tiles — for example, three 7-bamboo or three East winds.
  • A pair is two identical tiles.

So a complete hand might be: sequence + sequence + sequence + triplet + pair. Or: triplet + triplet + sequence + sequence + pair. Any combination of four sets (sequences or triplets) and one pair is valid. To win, you also have a valid yaku, which is covered in the Winning section below.

The tiles

A riichi mahjong set has 136 tiles:

  • Three suits (numbered 1–9)
    • manzu — "characters"
    • pinzu — "dots" or "circles"
    • souzu — "bamboo"
  • Honors (not numbered)
    • kazehai — "winds" (East, South, West, North)
    • sangenpai — "dragons" (White, Green, Red)

Each tile type appears exactly four times. Numbered suit tiles can form sequences or triplets; honor tiles can only form triplets.

Game Flow

Setup & rounds

Each player is assigned a seat wind at the start of a game: East, South, West, or North. The East player is always the dealer for that hand. Each player begins with 30,000 points — that's the standard at our club. The goal is to finish the game with more points than you started with, and ideally first at the table.

After a hand ends, the deal usually passes to the next player counter-clockwise and everyone's seat wind shifts with it — the South player becomes East (dealer), the West becomes South, and so on. The exception is when the dealer wins: they stay as dealer and the hand repeats. These repeated hands are called honba, and they add a small point bonus to the next win.

A round is complete once every player has had a turn as dealer. Rounds are named after winds — the East round is first, the South round is second. A full game (hanchan) plays both rounds, meaning each player deals at least twice. Some clubs and casual sessions play East-only games (tonpuusen) when time is short. We usually play full hanchan at club nights.

Your turn

Players hold 13 tiles. On your turn, you draw a 14th tile from the wall, then discard one to bring your hand back to 13. Turn order goes counter-clockwise. You're always working toward that four-sets-plus-one-pair shape described above.

When your hand is exactly one tile away from complete, you're in tenpai (ready). At that point, if your hand is still closed (no open calls made), you can declare riichi — place a 1,000-point stick on the table and announce it. Your hand locks in, but you gain a yaku (a scoring pattern required to win, covered below) and some exciting bonus tiles called uradora if you win.

Calling other players' discards

Instead of waiting to draw the tile you need, you can "call" certain tiles that other players discard to complete a set immediately:

  • chi — claim a discard to complete a sequence (only from the player to your left)
  • pon — claim a discard to complete a triplet (from anyone)
  • kan — claim a discard or use your own tiles to complete a quad of four identical tiles (from anyone, or self-drawn)

Making a chi, pon, or kan opens your hand, which locks out riichi and limits which yaku you can use. Staying closed keeps more options available, most importantly including riichi itself.

Winning

When you reach tenpai and your final tile arrives, you win by calling ron (winning off someone else's discard) or tsumo (drawing the winning tile yourself from the wall).

Yaku — the key requirement

A complete hand alone isn't enough to win. You also need at least one yaku — a recognized scoring pattern that makes the hand valid. Without one, you cannot declare a win even if your hand is complete. See the full yaku list on the Scoring page once you're ready for a complete reference.

Beginner yaku to learn first

  • riichi — declaring tenpai with a closed hand
  • tanyao — only tiles numbered 2–8 (no terminals or honors)
  • yakuhai — a triplet of dragons, your seat wind, or the round wind
  • toitoi — all triplets, no sequences
  • honitsu — one suit plus honor tiles
  • chiitoitsu — seven unique pairs (a non-standard hand shape: no sets, just pairs)

Scoring Basics

Every winning hand's value is built from two components: han and fu. Together they determine how many points change hands.

Han

Each yaku contributes one or more han, and a hand can combine multiple yaku. Bonus tiles called dora also add han, though they aren't yaku on their own — you still need at least one real yaku to win. The more han, the more valuable the hand.

Fu

Fu are base points earned from the specific shape of your hand and how you won. They add texture to lower-scoring hands. Once your hand reaches 5 or more han, fu no longer matter — the score jumps to a fixed limit amount. As a beginner, don't worry about calculating fu yourself — just ask a tablemate to help until it becomes familiar.

Limit hands

High-han hands earn special names and fixed payouts: mangan (5 han), haneman (6–7), baiman (8–10), sanbaiman (11–12), and counted yakuman (13+). At the top are true yakuman — rare, defined hands worth a staggering fixed amount regardless of han or fu.

Table Etiquette

Riichi mahjong has a set of common courtesies that keep games smooth and friendly. These aren't strict rules, but following them is good habit — especially at club nights and tournaments.

Tile handling

Keep your tiles upright and facing you. Don't touch other players' tiles, the wall, or the discard pond unless it's your turn or you're making a call. When discarding, place the tile firmly and clearly in front of you — avoid ambiguous placement.

Calls and declarations

Say your call out loud (chi, pon, kan, riichi, ron, tsumo) as you make it. Riichi is declared by placing a 1,000-point stick horizontally on the table before your discard. When you win, show your full hand face-up so everyone can verify it.

Pace

Try to keep things moving — long pauses slow the game for everyone. It's fine to think, but aim to be ready when your turn comes. If you need a moment for a complex decision, a brief "thinking" signal is appreciated.

Handling mistakes

Errors happen, especially when learning. Common ones include accidentally exposing a tile, making an invalid call, or mis-declaring a win (chombo). At club nights, your tablemates will help you sort it out — no stress. At tournaments, the rules for penalties are announced in advance.
