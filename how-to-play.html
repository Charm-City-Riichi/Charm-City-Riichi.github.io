How to Play

Overview

The goal

Mahjong is a four-player strategy game built on pattern recognition and timing. Each player draws and discards tiles to build a complete hand before anyone else does. A full game consists of several rounds.

There are many variants of mahjong, which are often named after the region that popularized them. Examples include Hong Kong Old Style and American Mah Jongg. Our club plays a Japanese mahjong variant called riichi, named for a particular move you can make during the game.

The tiles

A riichi mahjong set has 136 tiles:

  • Three suits (tiles are numbered 1-9)
    • manzu — "characters"
    • pinzu — "dots" or "circles"
    • souzu — "bamboo"
  • Honors (tiles are not numbered)
    • kazehai — "winds" (East, South, West, North)
    • sangenpai — "dragons" (White, Green, Red)

Each tile type (i.e. the four of bamboo or the East wind) appears four times.

Numbered tiles of the same suit can form triplets (i.e. 4-4-4) and sequences (i.e. 1-2-3 or 6-7-8); honors can only be used in triplets (i.e. E-E-E).

How a round works

Players have hands of 13 tiles. On their turn, they draw a 14th tile, then discard a tile to return to 13 in-hand. The turn order goes counter-clockwise.

You can "call" certain discards to finish a set of three tiles:

  • chi: a sequence, only from the player to your left
  • pon: a triplet, from anyone
  • kan: a quad, which is essentially an extended triplet

If you haven't made any calls, your hand is closed. A closed hand can declare riichi ("ready hand") when you're one tile away from winning.

Winning

You can finish your hand by calling ron (winning off someone's discard) or tsumo (drawing the winning tile yourself).

Structure

A standard winning hand has 14 tiles, with four sets and one pair:

  • A set can be a sequence (for example, 4–5–6 of the same suit) or a triplet (three of the same tile).
  • The pair is two identical tiles.

To win

You need both a complete hand and at least one yaku, which is a scoring pattern that makes the hand valid. Without a yaku, you cannot win!

Beginner yaku to learn first

  • riichi — declaring ready (closed hand only)
  • tanyao — only tiles numbered 2-8 (no 1s, 9s, or honor tiles)
  • yakuhai — contains a triplet of dragons, your seat wind, or the round wind
  • toitoi — only triplets (no sequences) plus your pair
  • honitsu — only tiles from a single suit, plus honor tiles
  • chiitoitsu — non-standard hand composed of seven unique pairs

Scoring

Every hand's value comes from two parts: han and fu.

Han

Each yaku adds one or more han, and hands can stack multiple yaku. Bonus tiles called dora also add han, though they aren't yaku on their own.

Fu

Fu are base points based on your hand's pattern and how you won. Fu are irrelevant if your hand scores 5 or more han.
